Forward Deployed Engineering: A Practical Field Guide

A practical playbook for finding AI-native workflows, earning trust from frontline teams, and turning field observation into deployable systems.

The first mistake in enterprise AI is asking, “What do you want to automate?”

Most teams cannot answer that question well. They know their work. They know their pain. But they often do not know which parts of the workflow are now technically possible to rebuild with AI.

That is why FDE, or Forward Deployed Engineering, matters. I do not think of it as simply “customer-facing engineering.” I think of it as a field method: observe real work, model the workflow, choose a narrow intervention, build in shadow mode, evaluate against real cases, and only then deploy.

The core move is simple: do not start with “AI features.” Start with the actual work people do under pressure.

1. Watch a real case, not a process map

If I were dropped into a company tomorrow, I would not begin by asking for a strategy deck. I would ask someone to open a recently completed ticket, investigation, sales proposal, claim, report, or customer request.

Then I would ask them to replay what actually happened.

Sometimes that does mean pulling up a chair. But it should not be passive watching. A useful field session has a mode, a boundary, and an evidence target.

Concrete operating mode

  • Sit beside the work. Stay slightly behind the user. Do not block the screen. Stay quiet for the first 10-15 minutes.
  • Record every switch. System A to system B, copy-paste, waiting, workaround, spreadsheet, Slack message, local note, re-check.
  • Ask only short questions. “Why did you open that?” “How did you decide?” “What would make this wrong?”
  • Work backward from artifacts. Start from the final output and reconstruct inputs, decisions, revisions, approvals, and rework.
  • Use logs to scale the observation. CRM, ticketing, ERP, Jira, ServiceNow, Slack, and workflow logs can reveal patterns that one shadowing session will miss.

The goal is not to watch people click buttons. The goal is to capture five things: where the input comes from, where judgment happens, where the action gets written back, who checks the result, and what happens when the system fails.

A 90-minute field session

0-10 min: set the relationship

Make clear that you are not auditing the person. You are looking for work the system forces humans to do.

10-25 min: watch silently

Record systems opened, pauses, copy-paste, side-channel messages, old emails, local notes, and spreadsheets.

25-55 min: ask short questions

Ask one action at a time: “Why did you switch from A to B?” “Is this fact-finding or formatting?” “What rule is in your head here?”

55-75 min: replay one case

Open a recently completed case and reconstruct it from final output back to input materials and approvals.

75-85 min: confirm the map

Say back the flow: “I think the painful part is evidence gathering in step 3 and narrative writing in step 6.” Let them correct you.

85-90 min: ask for samples

Request 5-10 anonymized examples: normal, complex, reworked, escalated, and ideal. No examples means no reliable evaluation.

Do not ask, “Where should we use AI?” Ask, “If an assistant prepared the evidence packet for this step, would you trust it? How would you check it?”

2. Look for AI-shaped friction

AI opportunities rarely announce themselves as “AI opportunities.” They show up as friction.

Cross-system lookup

A support agent copies a customer name from Zendesk, searches Salesforce for contract status, checks Stripe for payment history, then returns to Zendesk to write a reply.

Evidence gathering

An analyst reads PDFs, old emails, internal notes, and spreadsheets before writing a short recommendation.

Manual narrative generation

A compliance analyst turns structured facts into a report, a case summary, or an audit trail.

Unwritten expert judgment

A senior operator knows when a case “smells wrong,” but the rule is not captured in any system.

When you see cross-system work, draw the chain:

source -> key -> lookup -> decision -> writeback

Example: ticket ID from Zendesk -> account record in Salesforce -> payment status in Stripe -> refund recommendation -> note written back to Zendesk.

That chain is where an AI system can become useful: retrieval, summarization, classification, evidence packaging, pre-filled actions, quality checks, and escalation recommendations.

What you seeRecord immediatelyAsk nextAI opportunity
Copy a customer name from Salesforce and search Google Drive. Whether the source key is a name, account ID, email, or contract ID; how many searches it takes. “If search fails, where do you look next?” Entity linking, unified retrieval, evidence packet generation.
Open Excel, pull a number, paste it into a deck or report. Metric source, definition, freshness, owner, and review path. “Where does this number usually go wrong?” Auto-fill, metric validation, citation tracking.
Ask a teammate in Slack or Feishu. Who they ask, why the answer is not in the system, wait time, and whether the answer is written back. “How often does this question appear?” Knowledge-base repair, missing-field alerts, internal support agent.
Search PDFs or emails for a clause. Document type, page count, search terms, and relevance judgment. “What is the consequence of citing the wrong clause?” Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG: the model retrieves source material before drafting an answer.

3. Do not prototype before you have examples

A workflow is not ready for an AI prototype just because it feels painful.

Before building, collect a small evidence pack:

  • 20-50 real cases, anonymized if needed
  • Good outputs and bad outputs
  • Borderline cases
  • Examples that required escalation
  • Examples that caused rework
  • The human rubric for “acceptable” and “unacceptable”

This matters because an AI workflow without evaluation becomes demo theater. It can look impressive and still be unusable in production.

OpenAI’s enterprise deployments emphasize real workflows and custom evaluation. Anthropic’s agent work makes the same point from another angle: once agents use tools, remember context, and take multi-step actions, you need evaluations that test the whole trajectory, not just the final answer.

If you cannot sit beside the user

Screen-recorded self-narration

Ask the user to record one real case for 15 minutes while explaining decisions. One case, not a product demo.

Log sampling

Export 50-200 historical cases with created time, status changes, handler, close time, and reopen count. Start with the longest and weirdest paths.

Artifact packet replay

Ask for inputs, final output, comments, approvals, and rework versions. Interview around the packet instead of abstract process descriptions.

Expert/newcomer contrast

Interview a senior operator and a new user on the same workflow. Experts reveal tacit judgment; newcomers reveal learning cost and system confusion.

Use logs as objective evidence

Process mining means reconstructing real workflows from event logs. It does not replace field observation, but it prevents you from overfitting to one charismatic interview.

Minimal event log fields case_id The business object: ticket_123 / claim_456 / account_789 activity Created / Assigned / Reviewed / Reopened / Closed timestamp Minute-level or second-level time actor Person or anonymized role system Where the event happened status Current state metadata Optional: amount, risk tier, product line, customer segment

Start with five cuts: cycle time, wait time, reopen or rework rate, common variants, and manual handoffs. The best early targets often combine high frequency, long wait time, repeated rework, cross-system lookup, and clear judgment rules.

4. Score the workflow before choosing it

I would use six gates before committing to a workflow:

  • Value: Does this affect revenue, cost, risk, speed, quality, or customer experience?
  • Frequency: Does it happen often enough to matter?
  • Examples: Can we collect real inputs, outputs, failures, and edge cases?
  • Evaluation: Can a domain expert judge whether the AI output is right?
  • Integration: Can the system reach the needed data, tools, permissions, and work entry point?
  • Risk control: Can we start in shadow mode, add human review, log actions, and roll back?

The best first workflow is not always the biggest workflow. It is the workflow where value, examples, evaluation, integration, and risk control all exist at the same time.

Dimension1 point3 points5 points
Business valueConvenience only.Clear time saving or rework reduction.Revenue, risk, compliance, quality, or customer experience impact.

My threshold for prototyping: do not require the highest score, but require real examples, a person who can judge correctness, a reachable work entry point, and a safe failure mode.

5. Run a seven-day field cycle

A practical FDE discovery should move quickly:

Day 0Define one workflow, one team, one business metric.
Day 1Run 2-3 contextual inquiries.
Day 2Collect logs and artifact packs.
Day 3Score opportunities and choose one narrow workflow.
Day 4Create golden examples.
Day 5Build a shadow prototype.
Day 6Run 5-10 real cases with users.
Day 7Decide whether to pilot, gather more evidence, or kill.

The shadow prototype is the key. It lets the AI produce recommendations while humans keep control. You learn whether users trust the output before the system touches production.

6. Treat resistance as signal

When people do not cooperate, it is often not because they “do not get AI.” The incentives may be wrong.

  • If they only explain the official SOP, ask for a recent real case.
  • If they do not want to share examples, use anonymized samples and in-environment testing.
  • If experts hide edge cases, make them evaluation owners with veto power.
  • If IT blocks integration, start with shadow mode, least privilege, audit logs, and rollback.
  • If leadership only wants ROI, narrow the pilot to one metric.
Resistance patternLikely reasonPractical response
They only describe the official SOP. They fear being judged for workarounds. Ask for one recent case and say explicitly that the goal is to improve the system, not evaluate the person.
They refuse to share samples. Security, privacy, or unclear data boundaries. Use anonymized fields, redacted screenshots, synthetic structure, or in-environment testing where data does not leave their system.
Experts hide edge cases. They worry their know-how will be extracted or devalued. Make them evaluation owners with veto power. Position AI as reducing low-value work, not replacing their judgment.
IT blocks integration. Permissions, audit, support burden, incident risk. Begin with read-only access, least privilege, full logs, shadow mode, human approval, and rollback.
Leadership only asks for ROI. They do not want to fund exploration. Narrow the pilot to one metric, such as P95 cycle time, rework rate, or manual handoff count.

The right posture is not “trust the model.” It is: prove where the model deserves trust, and keep humans in the loop until it does.

7. Measure more than time saved

Time saved is useful, but it is not enough.

Business outcome

Cycle time, throughput, quality, risk reduction, customer experience.

User adoption

Active users, completed workflows, repeat usage, rejected suggestions.

System reliability

Tool-call success, failure counts, latency, cost, escalation rate.

Learning loop

Evaluation pass rate, edge-case coverage, feedback-to-fix time, transferability to another team.

For AI systems inside real operations, observability is not optional. It is part of the product.

8. The final deliverable should be concrete

After a field discovery, the output should not be “there are many AI opportunities.” That is too vague.

A real FDE deliverable should include:

  1. An as-is workflow map
  2. An evidence pack of real examples, logs, and user quotes
  3. A ranked opportunity shortlist
  4. Golden examples for evaluation
  5. An evaluation harness
  6. A shadow prototype
  7. A pilot plan with owner, metrics, permissions, and rollback

That is the difference between AI consulting and forward deployed engineering.

Consulting can stop at recommendations. FDE should leave behind a system, a measurement loop, and a customer team that can keep improving it.

One-sentence version

Forward Deployed Engineering is the discipline of using real field evidence to find high-value AI workflows, then turning them into evaluated, integrated, and adoption-ready systems.
