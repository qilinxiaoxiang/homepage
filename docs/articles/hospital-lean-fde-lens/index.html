What Lean in Hospitals Could Take From Forward-Deployed Engineering

I'm in a patient-safety fellowship held by the Jewish Healthcare Foundation, reading how hospitals try to land Lean. I found it is pretty much the same job as forward-deployed engineering — show up, map the real work, change it in small steps, make it stick. The difference is that FDE, the younger discipline, lands more often. So I flipped the usual direction: here is what healthcare can steal from us.

This session of my patient-safety fellowship is about putting Lean to work inside hospitals. I read the prep material expecting healthcare. What I found was a description of my own job.

Lean is the Toyota production method, transplanted from the factory floor to the hospital ward. Strip the clinical vocabulary and landing it in a hospital is the same shape as forward-deployed engineering: you go to where the work happens, you find out what actually happens there, you change it in small steps, and you fight to keep the change from sliding back.

That is the parallel. The more useful observation is the gap inside it. Both fields run the same loop, but they do not land at the same rate. Hospital Lean has a thin and contested track record. Forward-deployed engineering, two decades younger, routinely lands improvement work inside messy organizations. So the interesting question is not what the old discipline teaches the new one. It is the reverse.

Lean is mature in factories and struggling in hospitals. FDE is young and already lands in the wild. On the one problem they share — getting an improvement to survive in a complex human organization — the newer practice is ahead. That is worth stealing from.

1. The same job, two vocabularies

Forward-deployed engineering, stripped to its core, is on-site improvement work. You embed where the work is done, you find the friction, you build and land a change against it, and you stay until the change holds. Remote delivery and a slide deck do not count.

Now read Lean's own words for the same job. Go to the gemba — Japanese for "the actual place," the floor where the work happens. Map the value stream — the real end-to-end flow, not the org chart. Run kaizen — many small improvements, not one heroic redesign. And respect for people — the change is driven by the people who do the work, or it does not hold. Different vocabulary, identical instructions. The rest of this piece walks the loop one step at a time, then asks why one field runs it better.

2. You show up on the floor

Lean's first rule is genchi genbutsu: go and see the real thing. Do not diagnose a ward from a report in a conference room — walk it, watch a real patient move through it, time the waits yourself. The reason is the one every field rediscovers: the workflow managers describe and the workflow that actually runs rarely match.

That is the first line of an FDE engagement too. You do not start with "what would you like to automate." You start by sitting next to the person doing the work and watching the actual sequence — the copy-paste between two systems, the spreadsheet nobody mentions, the approval that takes three days. The friction you can build against is never in the documentation. It is on the floor.

3. You map the real workflow

Lean's signature tool is the value stream map: draw every step a unit passes through from entry to exit, and tag each step with how long it takes and whether it adds value. Draw it and one thing jumps out every time — most of the elapsed time is spent waiting, not working. A patient who waits four hours to be seen experienced four hours of pure waste.

For an engineer this is just profiling. The value stream map is a flame graph for a process: instrument the whole chain, and the hot segments — the waits, the handoffs, the rework — light up on their own instead of being guessed at. One study makes the point with numbers. Two clinics in Saudi Arabia mapped their real flow, found the waits, and redesigned: ophthalmology cut patient waiting by 89%, dentistry by 94.4%, without touching the care itself. The care was never the slow part. The waiting between care was.

4. You land one small change

Lean does not believe in the big-bang rollout. It believes in kaizen: a steady stream of small, reversible improvements, each one measured before the next. The discipline is in the measurement — an improvement that was not measured against a baseline does not count.

This is the iteration loop of any good deployment. Ship one small change against the friction you found, measure whether it actually moved the number, keep it or revert it, move to the next. Small steps are not a lack of ambition. They are how you stay reversible while you are still learning the system — which, on a hospital floor or a client's production environment, you always are.

5. You try to make it stick — and here both usually lose it

This is where the loop gets hard, and where both fields fail in the same way. You make the change, you watch the number improve, you leave — and the number slides back to where it started. The honest part of the Lean literature is that it publishes these failures instead of hiding them.

The diagnosis across the literature is consistent, and it is not "the theory is wrong." It is low fidelity: teams copy the visible tools — the kanban board, the value stream map, the week-long improvement event — and skip the invisible parts, the floor time and the frontline ownership. You can copy the part that photographs well. You cannot copy the part that did the work. FDE loses engagements the same way: ship the software, leave, and the new workflow quietly reverts to the old one because nobody on the inside ever owned it.

6. Why FDE lands more often

Same loop, same failure mode — but FDE lands the change more reliably than hospital Lean does. The reasons are structural, not a matter of one field trying harder.

A bounded unit with one owner

An FDE engagement is one workflow, in one team, that invited you, with a champion who feels the pain and has authority to change how the work is done. A hospital has no single owner of a value stream — and even "who the customer is" is contested across patient, physician, payer, and regulator. You cannot align a change nobody owns.

Tight, attributable feedback

FDE improvements are usually fast and legible: latency dropped, throughput rose, this many hours saved. Clinical outcomes are confounded and slow — the literature names "measurement and attribution" as a core difficulty. If you cannot prove the change worked, you cannot defend the budget to keep it alive.

Low stakes per change

Most FDE changes are software and reversible, so you can run the small-step loop fast. Every hospital change touches patient safety, which means slow approval and heavy regulation. The kaizen engine — many small reversible bets — is throttled exactly where the stakes are highest.

The work outlives the visit

The modern FDE model keeps an engineer embedded on the account, not running a workshop and leaving. Hospital Lean is too often delivered as an episodic "improvement event" by a team that then goes home — which is precisely the setup that produces the bounce-back.

7. What hospital Lean can take back

Two of those advantages are not laws of physics. They are practices, and practices transfer. If I were bringing something from forward-deployed engineering into a hospital improvement project, it would be these.

  1. Stay embedded and own a number — do not run an event and leaveThe single largest difference is persistence. FDE keeps a person on the account, accountable to one outcome metric, long after the launch. Replace the week-long "improvement event followed by a handoff" with a named owner who stays accountable to the catheter-infection rate, or the door-to-doctor time, for two quarters past the change. The bounce-back the literature keeps documenting is a staffing model, not a fact of nature.
  2. Encode the change in the system, not in human disciplineLean leans on training, posters, and standard-work documents — all of which decay as attention moves on. FDE's instinct is to build the change into a tool so the new way becomes the path of least resistance: the order set that defaults to the safe choice, the form that will not submit until the checklist is done. Make the standard work the default the software enforces, not a behavior people have to remember.
  3. Bound the unit and secure a champion before you startFDE does not try to fix a whole company at once. It picks one workflow with one owner who wants the outcome. Hospital Lean too often tries to boil the ocean across a system with no single owner. Start where someone with authority actually feels the pain, prove it there, then move — instead of launching enterprise-wide and diffusing the accountability to nobody.

The transfer runs both ways, and honesty requires the return trip. The one thing FDE should take from Lean is the principle it spent thirty years learning the hard way: respect for people. A change that is done to the people on the floor — software dropped on a team that never bought in — bounces back in software just as fast as it does in a hospital. The frontline owns the change or there is no change. That part Lean has always known. We rediscover it every time we forget it.

And the honest boundary on the whole comparison: hospital Lean's evidence base is genuinely thin — the most rigorous review of it concludes it falls short of a robust evidence base — and forward-deployed engineering is young enough that it has not earned a long track record either. I am not claiming FDE is the proven discipline and Lean the failed one. I am claiming something narrower. On the one problem both share — making an improvement survive inside a complex organization of people — the software-native practice has a better answer, and that answer is portable.

One-sentence version

Landing Lean in a hospital and forward-deployed engineering are the same job — show up, map the real work, change it in small steps, make it stick — and the younger field lands more often because it stays embedded and bakes the change into a tool instead of running an event and leaving.
